Currency Exchange and Convenient Payment Methods for Foreign Tourists in China

Posted on [Current Date] by Wander in China
Money Travel Tips

When traveling to China, understanding your currency exchange and payment options is crucial for a smooth and hassle-free experience. Here's a breakdown of reliable ways to exchange money and manage transactions during your trip.

Currency Exchange

At Airports:

  • Most major Chinese international airports, such as Beijing Capital International Airport (PEK), Shanghai Pudong International Airport (PVG), and Guangzhou Baiyun International Airport (CAN), offer currency exchange counters.
  • These are incredibly convenient, allowing you to exchange money immediately upon arrival. Counters typically have extended hours to accommodate international flight schedules.
  • However, keep in mind that exchange rates at airports are often less favorable than those found elsewhere. For example, exchanging USD 100 at an airport might yield slightly fewer yuan compared to a bank in the city center.

At Banks:

  • Major banks in China, including the Bank of China (中国银行), Industrial and Commercial Bank of China (ICBC, 中国工商银行), and China Construction Bank (CCB, 中国建设银行), provide currency exchange services.
  • In larger cities, you'll find numerous branches, especially in commercial districts. For example, Shanghai has many Bank of China branches along Nanjing Road.
  • To exchange currency at a bank, you'll need to present your valid passport for identification. Banks generally offer more competitive exchange rates. They also tend to handle a wider range of foreign currencies compared to airport counters or smaller bureaus.

At Authorized Currency Exchange Bureaus:

  • Authorized currency exchange bureaus can be found in popular tourist areas. For instance, you might see them on Beijing's Wangfujing Street or in Xi'an's Muslim Quarter.
  • These bureaus often have more flexible hours and can be more accessible than banks in certain situations.
  • However, it's essential to compare exchange rates, as they can vary between bureaus. Some may charge higher commission fees, so it's worth doing a little research beforehand.

Convenient Payment Methods

Mobile Payment Platforms:

  • Alipay (支付宝) and WeChat Pay (微信支付): These are the dominant mobile payment platforms in China. You can use them almost everywhere, from small street vendors to upscale department stores.
  • To use Alipay or WeChat Pay, you'll need to link a payment method, such as an international credit card or a bank account. Many major international credit cards are now supported, but compatibility can vary.
  • The process typically involves scanning a QR code provided by the merchant or allowing the merchant to scan your payment code within the app. For example, when buying a snack from a street vendor, you open the Alipay or WeChat Pay app, select the "Pay" option, and present your QR code for the vendor to scan. Payments are instant, and you'll receive a confirmation notification on your phone.

Credit and Debit Cards:

  • Major international credit cards like Visa, Mastercard, and American Express are accepted in many larger establishments, including hotels, restaurants, and shopping malls.
  • However, acceptance can be limited to smaller, local businesses. It's advisable to carry some cash as a backup, especially when venturing off the beaten path.
  • Debit cards with the UnionPay (银联) logo are more widely accepted in China, as UnionPay is the primary domestic payment network. If your home bank partners with UnionPay, your debit card should work smoothly at ATMs and point-of-sale terminals.

Prepaid Travel Cards:

  • Consider using a prepaid travel card. Some international financial institutions offer these cards, which can be loaded with Chinese yuan (RMB).
  • Prepaid cards offer the convenience of a debit card and can be used at ATMs and merchant terminals.
  • They also provide an extra layer of security, as they are not directly linked to your main bank account. You can typically reload the card as needed, and some offer features like fraud protection and online balance checks.

Final Recommendations

  • It's highly recommended to set up Alipay or WeChat Pay before your trip to China. Their near-ubiquitous acceptance across the country makes them the most convenient payment methods for most transactions. Linking your international credit card or bank account to these apps will simplify your payment process.
  • Carrying a small amount of cash is still a good idea. While mobile payments are widespread, cash may be necessary in very small shops, local markets, or for tipping. Having 500-1000 yuan on hand is generally sufficient, depending on your spending habits.
  • Bring a debit card that you can use to withdraw cash from ATMs. UnionPay-enabled debit cards are particularly useful due to their widespread acceptance at Chinese ATMs. This provides a reliable backup in case you need additional cash or encounter issues with mobile payment apps.
  • Credit cards are less strongly recommended as your primary payment method. While accepted in larger businesses, acceptance is not as universal as mobile payments or UnionPay debit cards. Additionally, international credit card transactions may incur extra fees, such as foreign transaction fees and currency conversion fees, increasing the cost of your purchases.

By following these tips, you can effectively manage your finances during your trip to China, allowing you to fully enjoy the country's rich culture, delicious food, and incredible sights without payment-related stress.
