Solving Phone and Internet Issues for Foreign Tourists in China

Posted on July 16, 2024 by Wander in China
Connectivity Travel Tips

Staying connected is vital when traveling in China. Whether it's for navigation, communicating with locals, or sharing your experiences, reliable phone and internet access is a must. Here's a practical guide to addressing potential connectivity challenges:

1. Buying a Local SIM Card

Where to Buy:

  • Airports: Most major international airports in China, such as Beijing Capital International Airport (PEK), Shanghai Pudong International Airport (PVG), and Guangzhou Baiyun International Airport (CAN), have kiosks or stores selling local SIM cards. This is a convenient option for getting connected right after you land.
  • Telecom Stores: In larger cities, you can find branches of China's three major telecom operators: China Mobile (中国移动), China Unicom (中国联通), and China Telecom (中国电信). These stores are usually located in busy commercial areas. For example, in Beijing, you can find them on Wangfujing Street or Xidan; in Shanghai, Nanjing Road is a good place to start your search.

What to Prepare:

  • Passport: You'll need to present your valid passport for identity verification when purchasing a SIM card. This is a standard requirement in China.

Choosing the Right Plan:

  • Each operator offers various plans. If your primary need is data for navigation and using apps, look for data-heavy plans. For example, China Mobile might offer a plan with 20GB of data for a month at a reasonable price. If you anticipate making local calls, choose a plan that includes both data and call minutes. China Unicom might have a package with 10GB of data and 200 local call minutes.

2. Using International Roaming

Contact Your Home Carrier:

  • Before traveling to China, contact your mobile carrier at home. Inquire about their international roaming options in China. Some carriers may offer specific roaming packages for China. For instance, a European carrier might have a package that allows you to use a certain amount of data and make calls at a reduced rate while in China.

Set Up Roaming:

  • Once you have the details, follow your carrier's instructions to activate international roaming on your phone. This is usually done through your phone's settings. However, be aware that international roaming charges can be very high, especially for data. Monitor your usage closely to avoid unexpected and costly bills.

3. Renting a Mobile Wi-Fi Device (MiFi)

Airport Rental:

  • Similar to SIM card kiosks, many airports in China offer mobile Wi-Fi (MiFi) device rentals. This is a good option if you need to connect multiple devices, such as your phone, tablet, and laptop. You can pick up the device upon arrival and return it at the airport before departure.

Online Rental:

  • You can also rent a MiFi device in advance through online platforms. Some companies allow you to book the device before your trip and have it delivered to your hotel in China. This offers the convenience of having the device ready upon check-in. The cost typically depends on the rental duration and the data allowance. For example, a week's rental of a MiFi device with a 10GB data limit might cost around $30-$40.

4. Using Public Wi-Fi

Cafes and Restaurants:

  • Many cafes and restaurants in China offer free Wi-Fi to customers. Popular chains like Starbucks, Costa Coffee, and local Chinese cafes usually provide Wi-Fi access. You can ask the staff for the Wi-Fi password when you order. However, the speed and reliability of public Wi-Fi in cafes can vary. Also, some establishments may require a Chinese mobile phone number for a verification code before you can connect. If this happens, ask the staff if there's an alternative way to access the Wi-Fi, such as a guest account. Some cafes may have a separate guest Wi-Fi network that doesn't need phone number verification.

Shopping Malls and Airports:

  • Shopping malls and airports also offer public Wi-Fi. In large shopping malls in major cities, you can often connect to the mall's Wi-Fi network. Airports usually provide free Wi-Fi for a limited time, which is useful upon arrival or while waiting for your flight. However, public Wi-Fi networks may not be as secure as private networks, so avoid conducting sensitive transactions like online banking when using them. Similar to cafes, the mall or airport Wi-Fi might ask for a Chinese mobile number for verification. If you encounter this, look for signs or ask at the information desk if there's a way to bypass this requirement or use a general guest access option.

By utilizing these methods, you can ensure reliable internet access throughout your trip to China, making your travels more convenient and enjoyable.
