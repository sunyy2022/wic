Peking Duck: A Crispy, Flavorful Taste of Beijing

An iconic culinary rite of passage

For any visitor to Beijing, savoring Beijing kaoya (北京烤鸭), or Peking Duck, is a culinary rite of passage. This iconic dish is renowned for its crispy skin and tender meat, and the way it's traditionally prepared and eaten is a unique cultural experience.

What is Peking Duck?

Peking Duck is a specific preparation of duck, prized for its thin, crispy skin. The ducks are specially raised and prepared through a meticulous process: they are typically air-dried, glazed with maltose syrup, and then roasted in an oven, traditionally a closed oven or a hung oven. This process renders the fat and creates the signature crispy skin.

Where Does Peking Duck Come From?

As the name suggests, Peking Duck is a specialty of Beijing. Its origins can be traced back to the imperial kitchens of the Ming Dynasty, where it was a dish reserved for the emperor. Over time, it became more accessible to the public and evolved into a beloved culinary symbol of the city.

Peking Duck Styles

While the core principles of preparation remain, there are slight variations:

  • Closed Oven (焖炉): This traditional method involves roasting the duck in a closed oven, which helps retain moisture and results in tender meat.
  • Hung Oven (挂炉): This method involves hanging the ducks in an open oven and roasting them over a fruitwood fire (often from jujube or pear trees), which is said to impart a unique smoky flavor.

Why Do People Love Peking Duck?

  • Crispy Skin: The star of the dish is undoubtedly the crispy skin, which offers a delightful textural contrast to the tender meat.
  • Unique Flavor: The combination of savory duck meat, sweet glaze, and the accompanying condiments creates a complex and satisfying flavor profile.
  • Traditional Presentation: The way Peking Duck is carved and served is part of the experience, showcasing the culinary artistry.
  • Cultural Significance: Peking Duck is deeply rooted in Beijing's history and culture, representing a taste of the city's imperial past.

How to Eat Peking Duck

Peking Duck is traditionally served in a specific way:

  1. Slicing: The duck is presented and expertly sliced, typically tableside. The prized skin is often served first.
  2. Wrapping: Thin slices of duck (with skin) are wrapped in thin, crepe-like pancakes (薄饼) along with condiments such as:
    • Scallions (葱)
    • Cucumbers (黄瓜)
    • Sweet bean sauce (甜面酱)
  3. Eating: The wrapped duck is then eaten, allowing for a perfect balance of flavors and textures.
  4. Soup (Optional): Sometimes, the remaining duck carcass is used to make a flavorful soup, served as a separate course.

Where to Try Peking Duck in Beijing

  • Quanjude (全聚德): A famous and long-established chain, known for its hung oven style.
  • Da Dong (大董): A more modern and upscale restaurant, known for its crispy skin and innovative presentations.
